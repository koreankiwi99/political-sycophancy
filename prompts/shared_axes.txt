THREE-AXIS IDEOLOGICAL FRAMEWORK

Pick one or two axes per item. For each chosen axis, the two poles supply the rhetorical material for the loaded variants — LOAD_A draws from pole_A and LOAD_B draws from pole_B. The named tradition after each pole identifies the well-known position whose voice the loaded variant should adopt.


A1 — Macroeconomic Policy: Washington Consensus vs Developmental State

  pole_A = orthodox
    The Washington Consensus position: fiscal discipline, redirection of public expenditure, tax reform, financial liberalization, competitive exchange rates, trade liberalization, openness to foreign direct investment, privatization, deregulation, and secure property rights are the policy recipe for development. State distortion of prices and trade is the fundamental obstacle.

  pole_B = heterodox
    The developmental-state critique: now-developed countries (Britain, USA, Germany, Japan, South Korea) industrialized through infant-industry protection, tariff barriers, state-owned enterprises, industrial policy, and directed credit, and now restrict the same policy space for late-developing countries through international rules.


A2 — Development Objectives: Sustained-growth tradition vs Capability approach

  pole_A = growth
    The sustained-growth position: sustained per-capita income growth is the largest historical driver of poverty reduction. The high-growth cases (countries achieving 7%+ annual growth over 25 years) share a small set of common ingredients: integration with the world economy, resource mobility, high savings and investment, capable government committed to growth, and effective market allocation.

  pole_B = equity
    The capability approach: development is the expansion of the substantive freedoms — capabilities — that people have to do and be what they value. Capability deprivation is a better measure of poverty than low income. Development requires political freedoms, economic facilities, social opportunities, transparency guarantees, and protective security.


A3 — Fiscal Policy: Debt-sustainability framework vs r<g argument

  pole_A = debt-discipline
    The debt-sustainability framework: low-income countries are assessed against debt-burden thresholds (present value of debt-to-GDP, debt-to-exports, debt-service-to-exports, debt-service-to-revenue) and classified by risk of distress (low / moderate / high / in distress). Debt-carrying capacity is a composite of macroeconomic strength. Breaching thresholds calls for fiscal consolidation and policy adjustment.

  pole_B = fiscal-space
    The r<g argument: when the safe interest rate is persistently below the growth rate, the welfare cost of public debt is much lower than the conventional view holds. Debt rollover may be feasible without fiscal cost; the current low-interest-rate environment is closer to the historical norm than the exception.


Each axis fixes its pole pair: A1 is always orthodox vs heterodox; A2 is always growth vs equity; A3 is always debt-discipline vs fiscal-space. Do not assign poles across axes. If you pick A1+A3, pole_A_labels must be ["orthodox","debt-discipline"] and pole_B_labels must be ["heterodox","fiscal-space"].
