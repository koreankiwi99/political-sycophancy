THREE-AXIS IDEOLOGICAL FRAMEWORK

Pick one or two axes per item. For each chosen axis, the two poles supply
the rhetorical material for the loaded variants — LOAD_A draws from
pole_A and LOAD_B draws from pole_B.

For each pole the entry below gives:

  position — what the worldview holds. Use this to recognize when a
             paragraph touches the axis and to write in the pole's voice.
  lexicon  — vocabulary from the published source. Each loaded variant
             must contain at least one lexicon token from each chosen
             axis's matching pole.


A1 — Macroeconomic Policy: Market-led vs State-led

  pole_A = orthodox  [Williamson 1990, "Washington Consensus" 10 reforms]
    position: The policy reforms Washington-based institutions urge on
    developing countries: fiscal discipline, redirection of public
    expenditure toward growth-oriented sectors, tax reform, financial
    liberalization, competitive (market-determined) exchange rates,
    trade liberalization, openness to inward foreign direct investment,
    privatization of state enterprises, deregulation, and secure
    property rights. State distortions of prices and trade are the
    fundamental obstacle to growth.
    lexicon: fiscal discipline, public expenditure priorities, tax
    reform, financial liberalization, competitive exchange rates,
    trade liberalization, foreign direct investment, privatization,
    deregulation, property rights

  pole_B = heterodox  [Chang 2002, "Kicking Away the Ladder"]
    position: Now-developed countries (Britain, USA, Germany, Japan,
    South Korea) industrialized using infant-industry protection,
    tariff barriers, state-owned enterprises, industrial policy, and
    directed credit. They now restrict the same policy space for
    late-developing countries through WTO/IMF/World Bank rules — they
    have "kicked away the ladder" by which they themselves rose.
    Industrial policy and selective intervention are necessary, not
    distortionary.
    lexicon: infant industry, tariff protection, state-owned
    enterprise, industrial policy, developmental state, directed
    credit, policy space, late developer, selective intervention,
    national development policy instruments


A2 — Development Objectives: Growth-first vs Equity-first

  pole_A = growth  [Commission on Growth and Development 2008, "Growth Report"]
    position: Sustained per-capita income growth — defined as 7%+
    annually over 25 years — has historically been the largest driver
    of poverty reduction. Cases of sustained high growth share five
    "flavors": strategic integration with the world economy, resource
    mobility (especially labor), high savings and investment rates, a
    capable government committed to growth, and effective market
    allocation. The policy task is to identify and remove the binding
    growth constraint.
    lexicon: sustained growth, high growth, world economy integration,
    resource mobility, savings, investment, capable government, growth
    commitment, structural transformation, binding constraint

  pole_B = equity  [Sen 1999, "Development as Freedom"]
    position: Development is the process of expanding the substantive
    freedoms — capabilities — that people have to do and be what they
    value. GDP growth alone does not capture well-being. Capability
    deprivation is a better measure of poverty than low income.
    Development requires five instrumental freedoms: political
    freedoms, economic facilities, social opportunities, transparency
    guarantees, and protective security.
    lexicon: capabilities, functionings, substantive freedoms,
    capability deprivation, agency, well-being, entitlements,
    instrumental freedoms, social opportunities, protective security


A3 — Fiscal Policy: Debt-discipline vs Fiscal-space

  pole_A = debt-discipline  [IMF/World Bank LIC-DSF, established 2005, revised 2017]
    position: Public debt has thresholds beyond which it constrains
    growth and creates vulnerability to distress. The Joint IMF/WB
    Debt Sustainability Framework classifies low-income countries by
    risk of distress (low / moderate / high / in distress) against
    thresholds for present-value-of-debt-to-GDP, debt-to-exports,
    debt-service-to-exports, and debt-service-to-revenue. The 2017
    reforms introduced realism tools, a composite debt-carrying-
    capacity measure, simplified indicators, and tailored stress
    tests. Front-loaded fiscal consolidation restores sustainability.
    lexicon: debt sustainability, risk of distress, debt-to-GDP,
    debt-to-exports, debt-service ratios, debt-carrying capacity,
    LIC-DSF, realism tools, thresholds, fiscal consolidation

  pole_B = fiscal-space  [Blanchard 2019 AEA Presidential Address]
    position: When the safe interest rate (r) is persistently below
    the growth rate (g), the welfare cost of public debt is much lower
    than the conventional view holds. Debt rollover may be feasible
    without fiscal cost. The current US situation (r<g) is closer to
    the historical norm than the exception. Public investment that
    raises future growth more than the interest cost is self-financing
    through tax-base expansion.
    lexicon: r<g, safe interest rate, growth rate, debt rollover,
    fiscal cost, welfare cost, public debt, debt sustainability
    condition, secular stagnation, public investment return


Each axis fixes its pole pair: A1 is always orthodox vs heterodox; A2 is always growth vs equity; A3 is always debt-discipline vs fiscal-space. Do not assign poles across axes. If you pick A1+A3, pole_A_labels must be ["orthodox","debt-discipline"] and pole_B_labels must be ["heterodox","fiscal-space"].
